Welcome to Baton Rouge

Fun Facts

As the capital city of Louisiana, Baton Rouge is the heart and soul of the state’s eclectic culture.

With over 300 years of history, Baton Rouge has a colorful story to tell, which can be tasted through our culinary offerings, seen through the picturesque views of the Mississippi River and historical landmarks and experienced through the vibrant arts and culture scene.

In the “Red Stick,” every day is worth celebrating, even more so through our festivals and events held throughout the year. So, sit back, relax, and let us make the planning easy – we’ll be waiting to welcome you with Southern hospitality and an authentic Louisiana experience!

European Settlement

French colonists established a trading post and military in 1721 at the site of a Muscogee boundary marker called a bâton rouge, or "red stick". The city's name comes from this marker.

Spanish Influence

In 1779, the Spanish defeated the English at Fort Butte and captured Baton Rouge. During Spanish Colonial occupation, the city was part of West Florida and a fort called Fort San Carlos was built on the river.

The Spanish allowed French settlers to maintain their language and customs, but in 1766, the first Spanish colonial governor, Don Antonio de Ulloa, imposed trade restrictions that angered the settlers. In 1768, the settlers rebelled and forced de Ulloa out, but Spain retook the colony in 1769.

In 1805, the Spanish Town District was founded, which is now the oldest neighborhood in the city and has the oldest architecture.

Civil War

Baton Rouge was under Union control for most of the Civil War, except for a brief period in 1862.

Other Events

In 1810, local settlers overthrew the Spanish and declared the area the West Florida Republic. Baton Rouge was incorporated in 1817 and became the state capital in 1849.

In 1846, the city replaced New Orleans as the state capital and construction began on the Neo-Gothic capitol building, which is now a National Historic Landmark. Baton Rouge was also the site of the first major boycott of the South during the Civil Rights Movement, when African Americans refused to ride segregated buses. The boycott sparked a national conversation and led to other notable protests.
